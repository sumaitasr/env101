Introduction




As a group, we participated in a citizen science project called Nature’s Notebook. Over the course of 3-4 weeks, we collected data tracking the change in tree foliage. We contributed to a project that helps scientists track changes in the timing of plant seasonal activity.

The Value and Impact of Participatory Science




Participatory science engages non-scientists in the process of collecting data. Getting the general public involved in the science community raises awareness of problems that the scientific community is trying to solve. Participatory science is also beneficial to scientists because they gain a wide range of data to further evaluate. If scientists were solely collecting data, they would have a smaller amount of data to work with. But through engaging non-scientists, there is a larger amount of data to analyze, resulting in more accurate conclusions.


Nature's Notebook was extremely user-friendly. We didnt have any training for the data collection yet, it was very easy to use. Data collection applications are specifically designed to be easy to use and not intimidating since science is for everyone. As such it is very possible to implement participatory science in all age groups. Introducing participatory science to younger children helps foster curiosity and scientific understanding. It would be extremely beneficial to children's academic growth to show science through simple experiments. Also, the added time outside would promote social and physical well-being.

The Importance of Our Project’s Topic

Overall, trees are crucial to every ecosystem regardless of location and urbanization. As such it is important to track the health of the trees, one way being tracking the phenological changes throughout the seasons. Phenological changes are shifts in the timing of important life cycle events, such as flowering, and leaf fall, in response to climate change. In the Fall, trees are known to change from vibrant green to reds, oranges, and yellows. Our job was to track these changes with climate change in mind. The timeframe of the leaves changing color has been shifted to changing later in Fall. While it may seem like a small detail, this is very important. Many organisms rely on the leaves changing color and falling. Fallen leaves are called leaf litter. Leaf litter contributes to nutrient cycling, and provides shelter and habitats for many animals. Finally, leaf litter creates a layer of organic matter that contributes to a healthy soil structure. The topic of observing phenological changes’ response to climate change is extremely important for ecosystem health.
